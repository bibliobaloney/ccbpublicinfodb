V. CLAIMANT RESPONDENTS The Copyright Claims Board finds that your claim, as amended, still does not comply with the requirements of the CASE Act and related regulations. The claim cannot move forward unless it is properly amended. If you wish to proceed with this claim, you must file a second amended claim by August 14, 2024. If you do not, the Board must dismiss your claim without prejudice, although you may file again in the future before the Board or in federal court. There is no additional filing fee for an amended claim. If you file a second amended claim and it is found compliant, you will be notified and directed to proceed with service. However, if your second amended claim also is found not to comply, the Board will dismiss the proceeding without prejudice. 17 U.S.C. § 1506(f)(1)(B); 37 C.F.R. § 224.1(d). To make your second amended claim compliant, you must resolve the issues identified below. 37 C.F.R. § 224.1(c)(2). You may also choose to correct or edit any errors or other information in your amended claim before you file it again. You do not need to provide a legal argument in your claim – just a statement of facts and circumstances. Being specific in your description gives the other party and the Board more information about your claim. There is no character limit, so please be as detailed as possible. Infringing Activity Your copyright infringement claim still does not provide enough facts about how the respondents engaged in infringing activity. It is not clear what aspects of your copyright-protected authorship you allege they infringed. In the “Works infringed” section, you list a photographic work titled “Own the Avenue Website,” which you describe as “[p]hotographs of my website and my intellectual property for OwnTheAvenue.com.” You list Registration No. VA 2-379-026 for the work. The Certificate of Registration for that work states, “Registration does not extend to any useful article depicted. Registration extends to deposited photographs only.” Your registration appears to cover photographic images on a website that depicts hundreds of articles of merchandise, mainly license plate frames bearing different short messages. However, your registration does not cover the content or design of any merchandise depicted in the photographs. It covers only photographic authorship. Distributing or displaying images of different license plate frames, even ones with the same messages or the same general appearance as the ones depicted in your images, would not by itself infringe that authorship. Photographic authorship is the creative expression created in an original photograph, not in the subject of the photograph. Elements of photographic authorship may include, among other things, the choice of lens, shutter speed, film, and filters; camera angle and perspective; positioning and layout; lighting and shading; background selection; digital alterations and enhancements; and photographic printing. More information about copyrightable authorship in photographs is available in Section 909 of Chapter 900 of the Compendium of U.S. Copyright Office Practices, Third Edition. Your amended claim does not clearly identify the creative expression in your registered work that you claim was infringed. Your general allegation that “these photos were created and composed by me” does not provide enough information. In other allegations, and supplemental documents filed with the amended claim, you indicate that the respondents copied or altered certain images of merchandise that appear in your registered work to make their merchandise, and you emphasize similarities in the appearance of the respondents’ license plate frames. However, it is not apparent what aspects of your photographic authorship the respondents used, as opposed to the content or designs that appear on the merchandise shown in your photographs. As stated in the June 14, 2024 noncompliance order: “Some of the supplemental documents filed with your claim leave it unclear if the respondents actually infringed the photographic images themselves, for example, by directly copying, distributing, or displaying the registered images, or altered versions of those images.” To address this issue in a second amended claim, you must identify elements of the photographic authorship covered by your copyright registration, such as those listed above in this order, and state facts that indicate how the respondents used those elements in their allegedly infringing works. You should be as detailed as possible, setting forth what the respondents did in relation to the work you allege was infringed.
