Copyright Ownership Your claim provides conflicting information about claimant LaKenya L Manahan’s (Manahan) status as a legal or beneficial owner of the allegedly infringed work. To bring a copyright infringement claim before the Board, the claimant must be “the legal or beneficial owner of the exclusive right [in a copyrighted work] at the time of the infringement.” 17 U.S.C. § 1504(c)(1). A legal owner is an author of the work who owns the exclusive rights at issue or a person or entity that has received a transfer of those rights, for example, by an assignment or an exclusive license. A beneficial owner is a legal owner who has transferred the rights at issue but retains some of the advantages of ownership, such as the right to receive royalties from the use of the work. You allege infringement of a motion picture or audiovisual work titled “VLOGMAS | Day 1-3 ‘You Can’t Save Me for Later’ | Rage Room + Oxygen Facial,” which you describe as a “Youtube Documentary Video Log.” You list Peyton Maree Charles (Charles) as the author of the work, and 1-14910934240 as the service request number of that work. Copyright Office records show that the pending application to register the copyright lists Charles as the sole author and copyright claimant (owner) of the work. A claimant who is not an author or named copyright owner of the allegedly infringed work must provide some specific details or supplemental documents explaining how the claimant owns, or has an exclusive license to use, the exclusive rights at issue. Or, if Charles and Manahan are actually the same person and use one of the names as a pseudonym or alias, you should clarify that relationship by including one of the names in the “Alias” field of the Claimant section. Substantial Similarity Your infringement claim does not include enough facts that explain the similarities between your work and the respondent’s allegedly infringing works. If it is not readily apparent from supplemental documents that are submitted with the claim, you must provide some specific allegations about how your work and the respondent’s works are substantially similar. In the “Describe the infringement” section of the claim, you state that “Respondent used several clips from my published content on Youtube without consent” and “Respondent has repeatedly used my content.” However, your claim does not provide detailed information describing how any of respondent’s videos are substantially similar to your work. For example, you do not identify which clips from your work were used, or how many, or for how long. In addition, you provided a copy of your work as a supplemental document, but you have not provided the respondent’s work for comparison. As a result, the claim does not provide enough information for the Board to evaluate the similarity of the works. When you submit an amended claim, it must include more factual allegations that support the element of substantial similarity. Please provide more details and background regarding this element in your amended claim, including a clear, detailed statement regarding how much of your work was taken by the respondent in the allegedly infringing videos, or documents that are sufficient to show the similarities. If you include copies of the works at issue as supplemental documents, they must be clearly labeled and identified so they can be properly compared to each other. More information about the elements required to assert a claim of copyright infringement is available in the Starting an Infringement Claim chapter of the CCB Handbook.
